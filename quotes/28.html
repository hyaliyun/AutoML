question:How does mechanistic interpretability differ from interpretability?

answer:Interpretability in the context of machine learning and artificial intelligence generally refers to the ease with which a human can understand why a model made a certain prediction or decision. It reflects the overall transparency of a model, which can be critical for safety, ethics, trust, and debugging purposes. There are various dimensions and degrees of interpretability, and it plays a crucial role in applications where it's important to not just get accurate predictions but also to understand the underlying reasons for those predictions. Mechanistic interpretability is a more specific term that refers to understanding the inner workings of a model in a detailed, cause-and-effect manner. It goes beyond knowing what features are important for predictions (which can be gleaned from methods like feature importance rankings) to understanding how the internal mechanisms of the model lead to its outputs. For example, in a mechanistically interpretable model, you might be able to trace the impact of a specific input through all the layers of a neural network to the final prediction, understanding the contribution of each component in a precise way. In essence, while interpretability might explain that Feature A and Feature B are important for predicting Outcome Y, mechanistic interpretability would detail the entire internal process, demonstrating how Feature A and Feature B interact within the model's structure to lead to Outcome Y. Achieving mechanistic interpretability is often more challenging than general interpretability, especially in complex models such as deep neural networks, which are notoriously difficult to interpret due to their "black box" nature. Simpler models like decision trees or linear regression tend to be more interpretable on a mechanistic level because the relationships between inputs and outputs are more straightforward and transparent. Researchers are actively working on methods to improve the mechanistic interpretability of complex models, developing techniques like visualization of model internals, layer-wise relevance propagation, and model distillation to make these systems more transparent and understandable.
